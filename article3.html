What the Ground Teaches You That No Data Can

Five lessons from four years of field research across Bihar, Andhra Pradesh, and Rajasthan — on booth agents, weekly markets, political silence, caste arithmetic's limits, and what voters watch when they think no one is watching.

By Nitesh Mishra
January 2026
9 min read

There is a conversation that happens in every post-election analysis room in India: why did the data miss this? A poll that showed 52% support for the incumbent translates into a 15-seat loss. A constituency where the field team reported "solid" returns by 40% margin. The analyst shrugs, cites the margin of error, and updates the model for the next cycle.

I have spent four years being present in a way that most analysts are not. Not reading the data. Being there — at village meetings in Seemanchal at 11pm, at weekly haats in Rajasthan on Tuesday mornings, in the booth agent's house three weeks before polling day in Vizianagaram. These five observations are things I could not have learned from any dataset. They are not anecdotes — they are structural patterns that repeat across states, communities, and election cycles.

Lesson OneThe Booth Agent Is India's Most Underrated Political Intelligence Asset

The academic literature on Indian elections is extensive and sophisticated. Lokniti-CSDS alone has produced decades of National Election Studies, State Assembly surveys, and youth studies that are genuinely world-class. What this literature almost entirely ignores is the booth agent.

A good booth agent — one who has worked the same 1,200-voter booth for 10 to 15 years — carries a map in his head that no electoral database can replicate. He knows not just how a household has voted historically but why their position may have shifted. He knows that the eldest son of a particular family left for Surat three months ago and will not return for polling. He knows that a land dispute between two OBC families has fractured the booth's internal cohesion. He knows that the local pradhan has switched allegiance — not publicly, but through the language of silence and absence.

Field Observation — Bobili, AP, March 2024

Three weeks before polling in Bobili constituency, a booth agent in a border mandal flagged an unusual quietness from a cluster of families who had historically been reliable TDP supporters. No aggression, no complaints — just absence from the usual pre-election conversations. Targeted outreach revealed a hyper-local grievance: a local contractor awarded a road-resurfacing project had excluded labourers from this specific community. A resolution — acknowledgement from the party's local coordinator and a commitment to address it — shifted the conversation. The seat was decided by a margin that made every booth count.

The booth agent doesn't produce reports. He doesn't use data tools. His intelligence is oral, relational, and real-time. The challenge for any campaign serious about winning is to create a system that extracts, records, and acts on what he knows. Most campaigns fail at this — not because they lack field workers, but because they lack the institutional systems to convert field knowledge into strategic decisions in time to matter.

For analysis of booth-level operations in Indian elections, see: Pradeep Chhibber & Rahul Verma, "Ideology and Identity: The Changing Party Systems of India," Oxford University Press, 2018; Lokniti-CSDS, State Assembly Election Studies, 1995–2019

Lesson TwoThe Weekly Market Is a Better Mood Tracker Than Any Poll

Every district in rural India has a haat — a weekly market where people from 10 to 15 villages converge. In Bihar's Seemanchal, it falls on different days across different blocks. In Rajasthan's Bikaner division, Tuesdays in Khajuwala are when the constituency's real political conversations happen — not at the rally, where the faithful attend, but at the grain merchant's stall, at the chai shop, and at the edges of the vegetable market where the conversation drifts without agenda.

The formal voter interview — structured questionnaire, recorded response — produces answers that people believe are socially appropriate. At the haat, the question is never asked. The answer is volunteered, unsolicited, mid-sentence about something else.

Field Observation — Khajuwala, Rajasthan, October 2023

Formal survey data across Khajuwala constituency showed Congress performing adequately on water scarcity — the dominant issue in the desert region. Three consecutive Tuesday visits to the main haat revealed something the survey had missed entirely: water had already been priced in by voters. It was a given, a chronic, an expected failure. The actual live issue was a ration shop contractor dispute — a specific individual whose conduct had become a proxy for everything voters disliked about the sitting MLA. This intelligence shifted how the final week's resources were allocated. It did not appear in any poll.

Survey methodology in India has inherent limitations in rural constituencies. The CSDS-Lokniti pre-poll survey for 2024 covered 10,019 individuals across 400 polling stations — remarkable scale and rigour. But even with this reach, the haat captures a register of political feeling that survives below the survey's detection threshold: the casual, the half-expressed, the still-forming.

Source: Lokniti-CSDS Pre-Poll Survey 2024 methodology — 10,019 respondents, 100 parliamentary constituencies, 19 states; Business Standard, April 2024

Lesson ThreeSilence on the Campaign Trail Is the Most Important Signal You Will Miss

Campaign teams make the same interpretive error consistently: they treat low enthusiasm as neutrality. It is almost never neutrality.

In rural India, political migration — switching party allegiance — is socially costly. It can fracture community relationships, strain family dynamics, and invite retribution from local power structures. So voters who have decided to switch do not announce it. They go quiet. The silence is the signal.

In Seemanchal across multiple election cycles, I documented a specific pattern: three to four weeks before polling, in constituencies where a shift was underway, the early indicators were all absences. The party flag outside a historically loyal household disappeared. The self-help group members stopped attending party-affiliated meetings. The local power broker stopped being reachable by phone. None of these were visible in any aggregate data. All of them were visible to a field team trained to notice what had stopped happening.

The challenge this creates for real-time campaign adjustment is significant. A field team reporting only what is happening — rally attendance, booth agent count, vehicle distribution — will give you a positive picture right up until it isn't. The field reporting system must explicitly ask: what has stopped happening that was happening two weeks ago?

For analysis of voter behaviour signalling in Indian elections: Yogendra Yadav, "Understanding the Second Democratic Upsurge," in Francine Frankel et al. (eds.), Transforming India, Oxford University Press, 2000; author's field observations, Bihar, 2022–2023

Lesson FourCaste Arithmetic Sets Your Ceiling — Ground Intelligence Determines What You Reach

The Lokniti-CSDS analysis of 2024 Lok Sabha results confirmed what practitioners have long observed: caste remained the primary organising principle of Indian voting behaviour, with no major national-level shift in social group preferences. BJP held its upper-caste, middle-class, EBC coalition. The INDIA alliance made gains among Dalits and landed farming communities in specific states.

But this national-level stability conceals enormous sub-constituency variation. In Seemanchal, within any given OBC community, I documented three to four distinct voting patterns based on hyper-local factors that no caste-based model accounts for: whether the community's local leader had contested on a party ticket and lost; whether a sub-caste marriage alliance had created a political allegiance; whether a specific government scheme had been locally administered in a way that favoured one sub-group over another.

Caste arithmetic tells you the approximate ceiling of your potential vote in any given constituency — roughly how many members of community X are registered, historically how many have voted for your party, and what the swing risk is. It does not tell you how much of that ceiling you will actually reach on election day. Ground intelligence fills the gap between the arithmetic and the actual.

This is why parties that run entirely on caste arithmetic — deploying resources based on community size without sub-constituency ground intelligence — consistently underperform their theoretical ceiling in constituencies where the internal community dynamics have shifted. The model was correct. The ground had moved.

Sources: Lokniti-CSDS, "Sociology of 2024 Lok Sabha Elections," ThePrint, March 2025; Pradeep Chhibber & Rahul Verma, Oxford University Press, 2018; author's field research, Seemanchal, Bihar, 2022–2023

Lesson FiveVoters Watch What Leaders Do When They Think No One Is Watching

This is the most difficult lesson to operationalise, and the one most frequently underestimated by campaign strategists who focus on media narrative and official messaging.

In a constituency in Bihar I researched across 2022–23, the sitting MLA had a reasonable development record: road construction, school renovations, ration card coverage, scheme delivery. His booth numbers should have been solid. Formal survey data showed adequate satisfaction. But field research — not surveys, but sustained presence in the constituency's informal information networks — revealed a persistent, low-volume narrative circulating primarily through the barber's shop, the chai stall, and the evening gathering at the village pump.

The MLA's son had behaved with arrogance toward a family in the main village over a minor land dispute. The MLA had been informed and had done nothing. This information appeared in no poll, no social media account, no formal intelligence report. It lived entirely in the oral record of the constituency's informal life. And it cost him votes — not a majority, but enough to reduce his margin to a point where the opposition could target it effectively.

Voters in rural India have spent decades developing extremely refined instruments for evaluating whether a political leader actually regards them as human beings. They watch small moments — how he walks through the market, whether he stops or keeps walking, whether his son calls back when someone leaves a message. The small things are the real things.

Data matters. I use it constantly, and AI tools have made my desk research significantly faster and more comprehensive. But ground intelligence is not a romantic addition to rigorous analysis — it is a practical requirement. The variables that determine election outcomes in India's constituencies operate at a level of granularity that no secondary data source can capture and no model can infer.

The analyst who reads the reports from Delhi will always be three steps behind the analyst who was at the haat on Tuesday.
