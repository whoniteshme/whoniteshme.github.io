The Revdi Republic: How Freebies Rewired the Indian Voter

From Madhya Pradesh's Ladli Behna to Bihar's record 71.6% women's turnout — how direct benefit transfers became the most powerful electoral instrument in Indian politics, and what the fiscal bill looks like.

By Nitesh Mishra
December 2025
11 min read

In July 2022, Prime Minister Modi coined the phrase "revdi culture" — free goods distributed before elections, irresponsible, fiscally dangerous, corrosive to democratic accountability. Three years later, the Bihar government run by his own alliance transferred ₹10,000 directly to 2.77 crore women weeks before the 2025 state elections. The NDA won 202 of 243 seats. Women turned out at a record 71.6%.

The revdi debate is dead. What replaced it is something more consequential: the systematic restructuring of how Indian governments relate to voters — and how voters evaluate governments. This piece examines the data behind that shift.

Origin PointMadhya Pradesh 2023: Where the Modern Template Was Forged

The contemporary era of freebie politics has a specific origin: March 2023, Madhya Pradesh. Chief Minister Shivraj Singh Chouhan, trailing in pre-election surveys, launched the Mukhyamantri Ladli Behna Yojana — ₹1,000 per month (later ₹1,250) deposited directly into women's bank accounts. Over 1.27 crore women enrolled — approximately 30% of MP's female population.

The BJP won November 2023 with 163 seats, up from 109 in 2018, significantly outperforming projections. Ladli Behna was widely credited by post-poll analysts as the decisive factor. The allocated budget for the scheme in 2024–25 was ₹18,984 crore. MP's fiscal deficit stood at 4.1% of GSDP — above the 3.5% FRBM ceiling.

The pattern across all six is identical: scheme launched 3–6 months before polling, targeted at women, delivered as cash to individual accounts via DBT infrastructure, and correlated with either significant seat gains or successful anti-incumbency resistance.

Bihar 2025The Most Consequential Data Point

Bihar 2025 is the case study that changes the frame most dramatically. It recorded its highest ever voter turnout at 66.9%. The gender pattern was definitive: women voted at 71.6%; men at 62.8%. In 130 of the 243 constituencies, more women voted than men. The NDA, which held 125 seats after 2020, rose to 202.

The ECPS analysis draws on ECI data and state budget documents to make a specific structural argument: Bihar 2025 demonstrates that DBT has created a new relationship between voter and state. The welfare scheme, once launched, becomes politically irretractable. Any government that cancelled it would lose the next election. Voters know this. And knowing it, they separate the act of gratitude from the act of voting.

"The Bihar election marks a turning point in Indian politics, with Direct Benefit Transfer schemes shifting from social support to powerful electoral tools. The unprecedented mobilisation of women voters, breaking caste patterns and strengthening loyalty to the ruling coalition, shows how material benefits can influence voting more effectively than identity-based politics." — ECPS Analysis, December 2025

The Fiscal RealityWhat the Bill Actually Looks Like

The political logic is impeccable. The fiscal logic is increasingly fragile. Across 11 states, an estimated 113 million women currently receive direct cash transfers of ₹1,000 to ₹2,500 per month. Five more states are in the planning or early implementation phase.

The fiscal costs are significant and compounding. Delhi's revenue surplus fell from ₹14,457 crore in 2022–23 to a projected ₹3,231 crore in 2024–25, partly attributable to welfare expenditure expansion. Annual revenue expenditure is projected to rise by ₹10,000–12,000 crore in states where freebies dominate election promises, according to fiscal analysis of state budget documents.

Maharashtra — which rode the Mazi Ladki Bahin to its 2024 victory — now faces state debt of ₹9.3 lakh crore with a revenue deficit of ₹45,891 crore. Karnataka's five Congress guarantees — free electricity, bus travel, rice, cash to women, and unemployment allowance — have collectively punched over ₹60,000 crore annually into the state budget, prompting property tax increases and cuts to capital expenditure in 2024–25.

Sources: Vajiramandravi Analysis, "The Freebies Debate in India," May 2025; State Budget Documents, Maharashtra, Karnataka, Delhi; Rajeshwari Deshpande, "Shaping of the Woman Constituency in Indian Elections," Studies in Indian Politics, Sage Publications, 2024

The Democratic QuestionWhat Freebie Politics Does to Accountability

The fiscal concern is well-documented. Less discussed is the democratic accountability concern.

When elections become primarily contests over who can deposit more money into more accounts before polling day, three structural shifts occur. First, policy reform becomes politically unaffordable — any reform that touches welfare delivery is a net vote loser, even if fiscally necessary. Second, accountability weakens — voters evaluate incoming transfers, not governance quality, roads built, courts that function, or water that arrives. Third, the baseline escalates — the RJD's counter-offer to Bihar's ₹10,000 transfer was ₹30,000. The next Bihar election will require more than the last one did.

The irony that anchors this entire debate: every party that condemned freebies as electoral bribery has, in power, launched competitive versions of the same. The BJP condemned the AAP's free electricity in Delhi; it then launched PM KISAN. Congress condemned Ladli Behna; it launched Gruha Lakshmi in Karnataka.

This is not hypocrisy so much as structural inevitability. Once voters have been conditioned to evaluate governments on benefit delivery, any party that refuses to play the game loses — not to moral superiority, but to the party willing to play. The revdi republic is not a phase. It is a new normal whose fiscal ceiling has not yet been tested.
